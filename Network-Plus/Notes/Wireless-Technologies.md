# Wireless Technologies and Standards

## 1. Standards and Naming Conventions
Wireless networking standards are developed by the **IEEE 802.11 committee**. To make these technical standards more accessible to consumers, the IEEE and Wi-Fi Alliance assigned simplified names to major releases.

| IEEE Standard | Wi-Fi Name | Generation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **802.11ac** | **Wi-Fi 5** | Previous Generation |
| **802.11ax** | **Wi-Fi 6 / 6E** | Current Standard |
| **802.11be** | **Wi-Fi 7** | Next Generation |

---

## 2. Frequencies and Channels
Wireless networks operate across specific frequency ranges, which are further divided into **channels** to simplify configuration.

*   **2.4 GHz**: Offers longer range but has only three non-overlapping channels (1, 6, and 11) when using standard 20 MHz bandwidth.
*   **5 GHz**: Provides higher throughput and many more available frequencies, reducing the likelihood of interference.
*   **6 GHz**: The newest allocation, providing the most bandwidth and the least amount of conflict with neighboring networks.



### Wireless Bandwidth
The "width" of the frequency used is the **bandwidth**. Common sizes include **20 MHz, 40 MHz, 80 MHz, and 160 MHz**. Larger bandwidths allow for faster data transfer but occupy more of the available spectrum.

---

## 3. Advanced Management Features
Modern access points (APs) use automated technologies to optimize the wireless environment without manual intervention.

*   **Band Steering**: Allows an administrator to "steer" a device that supports both 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz toward the higher-frequency 5 GHz band for better performance, rather than just relying on the strongest signal.
*   **DFS (Dynamic Frequency Selection)**: Part of the **802.11h** standard, this allows an AP to automatically identify and move to a channel that is not being used by other systems (such as radar or other APs).
*   **TPC (Transmit Power Control)**: Allows the AP to command a mobile client to reduce its radio power level. This minimizes interference with other nearby networks and services.

---

## 4. International Regulation
Wireless spectrum is a limited resource managed by national organizations, such as the **FCC (Federal Communications Commission)** in the United States.
*   **Interoperability**: Different countries have different rules for which frequencies can be used. 
*   **ITU (International Telecommunications Union)**: Provides global guidelines to help different countries manage wireless technologies and standards consistently.
